02

Inside the turbomolecular pump

At high vacuum the molecules fly freely and never collide, so the pump cannot push them like a fan. Instead, fast angled blades knock each molecule downward one bounce at a time. Because the trick depends on the blades matching the molecules' speed, light fast hydrogen is pumped far less effectively than heavy slow nitrogen, the reason hydrogen dominates what is left.

03

Pumping down to base pressure

No single pump spans the whole range. A backing pump clears the bulk air, a turbomolecular pump reaches the threshold of ultra-high vacuum near 10⁻⁷ Torr, and an ion pump crosses into ultra-high vacuum and holds the clock at 10⁻¹⁰ Torr. Press play to run the sequence — it drives the console above as it goes.

Stage: idle

The curve drops fastest at first, when there is plenty of gas to remove, then flattens as the system approaches the limit set by gas seeping from the walls. Near the end the dominant residual gas is hydrogen.

An educational model built as an output of my summer placement in the Time and Frequency division, CSIR-National Physical Laboratory. All quantities are computed from standard kinetic theory at 300 K; the turbomolecular-pump animation is a qualitative illustration of the molecular steering mechanism, not a fluid-dynamic solver. Equivalent altitudes use a standard-atmosphere profile and are approximate above 100 km, where the real atmosphere varies with solar activity. — Khyati Jindal
